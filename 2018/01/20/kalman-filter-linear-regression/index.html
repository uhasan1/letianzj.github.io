Kalman Filter Linear Regression

Posted on 2018-01-20 | In Machine_Learning , Deep_Learning |

Introduction

Kalman Filter is a state space model that assumes the system state evolves by some hidden and unobservable pattern. Instead we can only observe some measurable features from the system, based on which we try to guess the current state of the system. An example would be without openning your Bloomberg terminal, you try to guess stock market daily movement from the trader’s face who sits next to you. This book provides a simple random walk example where the hidden state is the true equilibrium stock price in some time interval while stock close price of that interval serves as an observation with errors. Then it proves that the true equilibrium price is the weighted average of close prices with the weights being Fibonacci sequence. Dr Chan make Kalman Filter popular to the quantitative trading online community with his EWA-EWC ETF pairs trading strategy.

In this post we will continue with our simple linear regression example from last post, and follow the plain Kalman Filter logic without the help of Python packages such as PyKalman.

Appendix – Mathematics

The state transition equation is

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
\theta_t &=& G_t\theta_{t-1}+W_t \
(p\times 1) & & (p\times p)(p\times 1)+(p\times 1)
\end{array}
$$

where $W_t \sim i.i.d\hspace{5pt} N(0,W_t)$

The measurement/observation equation is

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
y_t &=& F_t\theta_t+V_t \
(m\times 1) & & (m\times p)(p\times 1)+(m\times 1)
\end{array}
$$

where $V_t \sim i.i.d.\hspace{5pt} N(0,V_t)$

It needs initial values $\hat{\theta}{0|0}$ and initial covariance matrix $P{0|0}$. Then,

A-priori predictions of state and state covariance are

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
\hat{\theta}_{k|k-1} &=& G_k\hat{\theta}_{k-1|k-1} \
(p\times 1) & & (p\times p)(p\times 1)
\end{array}
$$

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
P_{k|k-1} &=& G_kP_{k-1|k-1}G_k^T+W_t \
(p\times p) & & (p\times p)(p\times p)(p\times p)+(p\times p)
\end{array}
$$

After the measurement/obersavation $y_k$, the update process is

(1) innovation or measurement residual

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
\hat{y}_{k} &=& y_k-F_k\hat{\theta}_{k|k-1} \
(m\times 1) & & (m\times 1)-(m\times p)(p\times 1)
\end{array}
$$

(2) residual covariance

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
S_k &=& F_kP_{k|k-1}F_k^T+V_k \
(m\times m) & & (m\times p)(p\times p)(p\times m)+(m\times m)
\end{array}
$$

(3) Kalman gain

$$
\begin{array}{lcl} K_k &=& P_{k|k-1}F_k^TS_K^{-1} \
(p\times m) & & (p\times p)(p\times m)(m\times m)
\end{array}
$$

(4) and Posterior estimation of state and state covariance

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
\hat{\theta}{k|k} &=& \hat{\theta}{k|k-1}+K_k\hat{y_k} \
(p\times 1) & & (p\times 1)+(p\times m)(m\times 1)
\end{array}
$$

$$
\begin{array}{lcl}
P_{k|k} &=& (I-K_kF_k)P_{k|k-1} \
(p\times p) & & (p\times p)-(p\times m)(m\times p)
\end{array}
$$

An example is given in the book Pairs Trading: Quantitative Methods and Analysis

Initialize, assuming $W=V=\sigma$

$$
\hat{\theta}_{0|0}=y_0\hspace{10mm}P_{0|0}=\sigma
$$

At step k=1, the prediction,

$$
\hat{\theta}{1|0}=\hat{\theta}{0|0}=y_0
$$

$$
P_{1|0}=P_{0|0}+\sigma=2\sigma
$$

After observation,

$$
\hat{y}_1=y_1-\hat{\theta}_{1|0}=y_1-y_0
$$

$$
S_1=P_{1|0}+\sigma=3\sigma
$$

$$
K_1=P_{1|0}/S_1=2/3
$$

$$
\hat{\theta}{1|1}=\hat{\theta}{1|0}+2/3(y_1-y_0)=2/3y_1-1/3y_0
$$

$$
P_{1|1}=(1-2/3)P_{1|0}=2/3\sigma
$$

Then at step k=2,

$$
\hat{\theta}{2|1}=\hat{\theta}{1|1}=2/3y_1+1/3y_0
$$

$$
P_{2|1}=P_{1|1}+\sigma=5/3\sigma
$$

Upon observation,

$$
\hat{y}_2=y_2-\hat{\theta}_{2|1}=y_2-2/3y_1-1/3y_0
$$

$$
S_2=P_{2|1}+\sigma=8/3\sigma
$$

$$
K_2=(5/3)/(8/3)=5/8
$$

$$
\hat{\theta}{2|2}=\hat{\theta}{2|1}+5/8\hat{y}_2=\frac{5}{8}y_2+\frac{2}{8}y_1+\frac{1}{8}y_0
$$

$$
P_{2|2}=(1-5/8)P_{2|1}=\frac{5}{8}\sigma
$$

The Fibonacci number pattern extends, for example,

$$
\hat{\theta}_{3|3}=\frac{13}{21}y_3+\frac{5}{21}y_2+\frac{2}{21}y_1+\frac{1}{21}y_0
$$
